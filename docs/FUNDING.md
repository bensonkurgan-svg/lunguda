# Funding & support guide

How to fund the Lunguda Heritage Archive — from international grants to your own community's elders and diaspora.

> **On the numbers below:** grant amounts, deadlines and contact details change every cycle. Figures here were checked in **mid-2026** and each entry links to the official page. **Always confirm the current round before applying.** Nothing here is legal or financial advice.

---

## 1. Position the project before you ask anyone for money

Funders give to a clear story, not a website. Decide your framing first:

- **This is preventive documentation + revitalisation.** Lunguda has tens of thousands of speakers across five dialects, but younger generations are shifting to dominant languages. You are documenting while the language is still strong *and* building a tool for children to learn it. That dual framing fits the widest set of funders.
- **The archive outlives the website.** Your strongest selling point: every recording, transcription and meaning can be **exported and deposited in a permanent archive** (e.g. ELAR). Funders fear paying for work that vanishes — lead with permanence.
- **It is community-owned.** Heritage content belongs to the Lunguda community. Say so. Indigenous-led, community-owned projects are explicitly prioritised by most language funders.
- **Anchor the urgency with a neighbour.** The same LGA contains far smaller, more endangered languages (e.g. Centuum, with very few speakers). Documenting the region — not just one language — strengthens "endangerment hotspot" applications.

Keep a one-page summary, a two-minute audio sample from the archive, and a simple budget ready. You will reuse them everywhere.

## 2. Best-fit grants (start here)

### a) Endangered Language Fund — Language Legacies *(most accessible)*
- **Why it fits:** open to **community members**, no academic degree required, any country; funds **both documentation and revitalisation**.
- **Amount:** up to **US$4,000**, averaging ~US$2,000; one-year grants (verify current cap).
- **Catch:** no overhead/admin costs covered; some languages are ineligible — check their eligible-languages list for Lunguda first.
- **Apply:** https://www.endangeredlanguagefund.org/language-legacies.html · email `elf@endangeredlanguagefund.org`

### b) ELDP — Endangered Languages Documentation Programme *(the major prize)*
- **Why it fits:** the leading documentation funder; **explicitly welcomes documenters from the language community** and local scholars/students, any nationality. Has awarded €25M+ since 2002.
- **Grant types & amounts (verify):**
  - **Rapid Grant** — small, rolling, decision within ~6 weeks. For *preliminary* work: first community engagement, word-lists to establish language status/classification, feasibility. **This is the ideal first ELDP application for you.**
  - **Small Grant** — up to **€10,000**, 6–12 months.
  - **Major Documentation Project** — €10k to **€300,000** (needs a host institution).
- **Important caveats:** ELDP funds **documentation, not revitalisation**, and requires you to **deposit your materials in the ELAR archive** (now in Berlin/BBAW). Major/scholarship grants require a **host institution** (e.g. a Nigerian university — see §5 on partnering).
- **Cycle:** annual round, **applications due 1 October**, results the following April. Rapid Grants anytime.
- **Apply:** https://www.eldp.net · email `eldp@eldp.net`

### c) Firebird Foundation — Fellowship for the documentation of oral literature
- **Why it fits:** funds recording of **oral traditions** (folktales, proverbs, songs) — exactly your Oral Traditions section.
- **Apply:** https://www.firebirdfellowships.org (verify current call and amount).

### d) Foundation for Endangered Languages (FEL)
- **Why it fits:** small grants for community-based documentation/revitalisation; modest amounts (historically around US$1,000) but low barrier and friendly to first-timers.
- **Apply:** https://www.ogmios.org/grants (verify current round).

### e) Living Tongues Institute — partner + free tooling
- **Why it fits:** runs the free **Living Dictionaries** platform and supports community language activists. Even where they don't grant cash, a partnership lends credibility and tools. Their funding-opportunities page is a useful directory in its own right.
- **Contact:** https://livingtongues.org

### f) Modern Endangered Archives Program (MEAP, UCLA)
- **Why it fits:** funds **digitising and preserving at-risk archives** — relevant if elders hold old cassette/photo collections you want to rescue into this platform.
- **Apply:** https://www.library.ucla.edu/meap (verify current call).

### g) Cultural Survival & National Geographic Society
- **Cultural Survival** funds Indigenous **community media** (e.g. radio) and language work — check regional eligibility for Nigeria each cycle: https://www.culturalsurvival.org/grants
- **National Geographic Society** funds storytelling, conservation and cultural documentation projects: https://www.nationalgeographic.org/society/grants-and-investments/

## 3. The UNESCO route (go through the government, not directly)

UNESCO's **Intangible Cultural Heritage (ICH) Fund does not accept applications from individuals or NGOs directly.** Requests must be **submitted by a State Party** — for you, the Federal Government of Nigeria.

- **What to ask the government for:** that Lunguda heritage be put forward for ICH **preparatory assistance** / inventory support, with your archive as the ready-made documentation base.
- **Who transmits it:** the **Nigerian National Commission for UNESCO** and the Federal Ministry of Tourism/Culture.
- **General ICH assistance contact:** `ICH-Assistance@unesco.org` · https://ich.unesco.org

Treat UNESCO as a long-game, prestige outcome that the Nigerian bodies in §5 unlock — not a first-year cash source.

## 4. Community elders & well-to-do individuals (your nearest, most reliable support)

International grants are slow and competitive. Your **own community's elders, leaders and diaspora** can fund recording equipment, data, transport and stipends *this month* — and their backing makes every external application stronger.

### Who to approach
1. **Traditional leadership** — the Chief of Jessu and the district/clan heads. Their blessing is both moral authority and a door-opener to everyone else.
2. **Community elders & custodians** — not always wealthy, but they give the knowledge and the legitimacy. Some will also contribute or mobilise others.
3. **Lunguda professionals & businesspeople** — civil servants, academics, traders, contractors, oil/agribusiness staff from Jessu and Gombe State.
4. **The diaspora** — Lunguda people in Abuja, Lagos, Jos, and abroad. They are often the most willing to fund "home" projects and the easiest to reach online.
5. **Hometown & alumni associations** — Lunguda/Jessu development unions, church and mosque communities, old-students' associations. These already collect dues and fund community projects.

### How to find and reach them
- Ask the traditional council and elders for an introduction list — **a referral from the Chief or an elder converts far better than a cold message.**
- Post the live archive URL in **community WhatsApp/Facebook groups** and hometown-association channels; let the work speak.
- Identify diaspora members through those same associations and LinkedIn; reach out individually.

### What to ask for (make it concrete and small)
People give to specific, finishable things — not "support our project":

| Ask | Rough cost | Names them as |
|-----|-----------:|---------------|
| A good field recorder + headphones | one device | Equipment sponsor |
| Monthly data/airtime for a field recorder | small monthly | Data sponsor |
| Transport to record an elder in a distant village | per trip | Fieldwork sponsor |
| "Adopt a dialect" — fund recording 100 words in Kɔla | a set sum | Dialect patron |
| Sponsor the hosting + domain for a year | ~one modest annual sum | Hosting patron |

Offer recognition: a **"Supporters" / "Patrons" credit** on the About page, naming individuals and associations (with their consent). Recognition is often the whole motivation.

### A short outreach message you can adapt

> **Subject: Help us save the Nʋngʋra language — a Jessu project**
>
> Dear [Name / Elder / Chief],
>
> We have built a free online archive of our Lunguda language and culture — words with the actual voices of our people, our proverbs, our rulers' history, and our family lineage. You can see it here: **[your URL]**.
>
> Our language is still strong, but our children are speaking it less. This archive records it now, in our elders' own voices, so it is never lost — and so the next generation can learn it.
>
> We are looking for a few people from Jessu to help. A gift of **[specific amount / a recorder / a year of hosting]** would let us record **[specific thing, e.g. 100 more words with audio, or one elder's full collection of folktales]**. Supporters will be named as Patrons of the archive, with your permission.
>
> May I call you to explain more? Thank you for standing with our heritage.
>
> [Your name] · [phone] · on behalf of the Lunguda Heritage Archive

### Keep it trustworthy
- Use a **dedicated account** managed by the community association, not a personal one, with at least two signatories.
- Publish a **simple running tally** of what was raised and what it bought. Transparency is what brings the second and third gift.
- Always get **consent** before naming a donor or publishing an elder's recording (the app already records a consent flag on each entry).

## 5. Nigerian institutions & NGOs to contact

These give credibility, possible co-funding, and the host-institution affiliation that the bigger grants require.

- **Nigerian National Commission for UNESCO (NATCOM)** — your channel to the UNESCO ICH route (§3). Federal Ministry of Education.
- **National Council for Arts and Culture (NCAC)** — federal body for cultural promotion and documentation; possible partner/endorser. https://ncac.gov.ng
- **National Institute for Cultural Orientation (NICO)** — runs language and cultural-heritage programmes; natural ally for a language archive.
- **Gombe State Ministry of Culture & Tourism** — your state government; seek endorsement and small support, and a letter that strengthens federal/international applications.
- **A Nigerian university linguistics department** — e.g. the University of Jos, Gombe State University, or the University of Maiduguri. A department can act as the **host institution** ELDP's larger grants require, and supply academic mentorship. This is often the single most valuable partnership you can form.
- **TETFund-funded research** (via a partner university) — if an academic collaborator folds Lunguda documentation into a funded research project.
- **Pan-African / international NGOs:** Cultural Survival (§2g), the **Endangered Languages Project** (catalogue + visibility, endangeredlanguages.com), and **PanAfriL10n / African language-tech networks** for digital tooling partnerships.

## 6. A simple sequence to follow

1. **This month:** get the Chief's and elders' blessing; raise a small local/diaspora fund for equipment and data (§4). Put live the archive URL everywhere.
2. **This quarter:** apply for an **ELDP Rapid Grant** and an **ELF Language Legacies** grant (§2a–b) — both welcome community applicants and need no institution for the entry level.
3. **In parallel:** partner with a **Nigerian university linguistics department** (§5) for mentorship and a host affiliation.
4. **Within the year:** with that partner, target an **ELDP Small/Major** grant and approach **Gombe State + NATCOM** for endorsement and the UNESCO inventory route.
5. **Ongoing:** keep depositing exported data to a permanent archive (ELAR) — it satisfies funders and protects the work forever.
